How to Use AI Properly

⏱ 15 min read 📅 Updated 2025 🎓 Beginner–Intermediate

What is Artificial Intelligence?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to computer systems designed to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence — such as understanding language, recognising images, making decisions, and generating creative content. Modern AI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Copilot are built on Large Language Models (LLMs) — trained on enormous datasets to predict and generate human-like text.

AI is now embedded in everyday tools: search engines, email filters, social media feeds, navigation apps, grammar checkers, and more. Understanding how it works — and where it falls short — is essential for using it responsibly.

🤖

Generative AI

Creates new content (text, images, code) based on patterns learned during training.

📊

Predictive AI

Forecasts outcomes based on historical data — used in finance, medicine, and logistics.

👁️

Computer Vision

Identifies and interprets images and video — used in facial recognition and medical imaging.

🗣️

Natural Language Processing

Understands and generates human language — powering chatbots, translation, and summarisation.

Accuracy & Verification — Don't Just Trust It

AI language models do not "know" facts the way a database does. They generate responses based on statistical patterns — which means they can confidently state things that are completely wrong. This phenomenon is called hallucination.

Definition — Hallucination: When an AI generates information that sounds plausible and confident but is factually incorrect, made-up, or misleading. AI models do not know when they are wrong.

AI also has a knowledge cutoff — the date when its training data ended. It does not automatically know about events, laws, or research published after this point. Always check dates when using AI for current information.

✔ Do

  • Cross-check important facts with authoritative sources (government sites, peer-reviewed journals, official publications)
  • Ask AI to cite its sources and verify those citations exist
  • Treat AI output as a starting point, not a final answer
  • Use AI for brainstorming, drafting, and summarising — then verify

✘ Don't

  • Submit AI-generated statistics or quotes without checking them
  • Trust AI for medical diagnoses, legal advice, or financial decisions
  • Assume confident-sounding answers are accurate
  • Use AI outputs as your only research source

Privacy & Data — What You Share Matters

When you enter information into an AI tool, that data may be stored, used to improve the model, reviewed by staff, or shared under the platform's terms of service. This has serious implications for personal, medical, financial, and confidential professional information.

⚠️ Never enter into an AI chatbot: your full name combined with sensitive details, passwords or security codes, medical records, financial account details, private communications, or confidential work data — unless your organisation has a verified, private AI deployment.

Many organisations and schools have explicit policies about which AI tools are approved and what data can be shared. Always check your school's or employer's policy before using AI for work-related tasks.

Key Privacy Principles

  • Data minimisation: Share only what is necessary. If the AI doesn't need your real name to help you, don't give it.
  • Anonymisation: When asking about scenarios involving real people, replace identifying details with fictional names or descriptions.
  • Read the terms: Understand whether your data is used for training, how long it's retained, and whether you can delete it.
  • Use opt-out features: Many AI platforms allow you to opt out of data training. Enable this when possible.

Bias & Fairness — AI Reflects Human Data

AI models are trained on vast datasets scraped from the internet and other sources. Because those sources reflect historical human biases — including racial, gender, cultural, and socioeconomic biases — AI models can reproduce and amplify those biases in their outputs.

Examples of AI bias include: facial recognition systems that perform worse on darker skin tones; hiring algorithms that disadvantage women; translation tools that assume gender based on job titles; and AI-generated images that default to specific cultural representations.

Remember: AI bias is not always intentional. It is often an invisible reflection of unequal data. Being aware of it is the first step to catching it.

How to reduce the impact of AI bias:

  • Critically evaluate AI outputs, especially on topics involving people, culture, or demographics
  • Test AI tools with diverse inputs and compare results
  • Avoid using AI as the sole decision-maker in consequential choices about people (hiring, grading, lending)
  • Report concerning outputs to your teacher, employer, or the platform directly

Responsible Use — The Big Picture

Using AI responsibly means understanding its capabilities and its limits, being honest about when and how you use it, and making sure your use doesn't harm others. This is especially important in academic and professional contexts.

Academic integrity: Submitting AI-generated work as entirely your own is considered academic dishonesty at most institutions. Check your school's policy. In many contexts, AI can be used as a tool — like a calculator or spell-checker — but the thinking, analysis, and ideas must still be your own.

Over-reliance risk: Relying too heavily on AI can atrophy your own skills. Use AI to supplement your thinking, not replace it. Writing your own draft first, then using AI to refine it, is a healthier approach than asking AI to write everything for you.

Environmental impact: Training and running large AI models consumes significant energy. Being thoughtful about when and how much you use AI tools is a small but meaningful contribution to sustainability.

The Golden Rule of AI Use: If you wouldn't be comfortable telling your teacher or employer exactly how you used AI in your work, reconsider whether your use is appropriate.

When AI is genuinely useful:

  • Drafting and editing text (with your own critical review)
  • Explaining complex concepts in simpler language
  • Brainstorming and generating ideas
  • Summarising long documents
  • Writing and debugging code (with your understanding)
  • Translating language
  • Generating starting points for creative work
